Excerpts from OSHA Ergonomic Report (aa990126)
SIC 4512 - Air Cargo and Passenger Carriers, Scheduled
BAGGAGE HANDLING

Potential Hazards and Possible Solutions

  • Unloading baggage from the baggage carts onto the carousel conveyor (Figure 1) exposes Terminal Agents to the ergonomic hazards of repeated lifting, repeated torso flexions, torso twisting, and extended reaches while supporting a load in the hands. Employees at this station work at a very high rate of repetition and must reach and bend the torso because the carousel conveyor is at a low height of only about 12 inches. High frequency lifting, repeated torso flexions, reaching with a load in the hand, and twisting of the torso are all hazards which increase the risk of development of Musculoskeletal Disorders (MSDs) of the low back and shoulder.

Terminal employees who are unloading baggage from the baggage carts onto the carousel conveyor should be provided with a short roller conveyor which will bridge the distance between baggage cart and the conveyor. Figure 5 shows appropriate placement of this type of device. This type of device could be anchored to the edge of the conveyor and allowed to slide in a manner similar to that used that used for library access ladders. The free end could then be laid onto the edge of the cart. Employees could pull the bag to the bridge conveyor where gravity would allow it to slide down to the takeaway area. Using this type of device would allow employees to lift loads with little torso flexion, limit long reaches while supporting a load, and limit lifting since most lifts could be transformed into slides.

 

  • Customer Service Agents must repeatedly lift and carry heavy baggage items from the area where customers deposit them (Figure 2) to the conveyor that takes them to the cart loading area (Figure 3). These bags can weigh up to or exceed about 70 pounds. This task involves deep torso flexions, twisting, and reaching with a load in the hands. Repeatedly lifting heavy items utilizing torso flexions and twisting and reaching with a load in the hand are hazards which increase the risk of development of (MSDs) of the low back and shoulder.

 

 


 

  • All baggage should be weighed at the point where it is deposited by the customer and heavier pieces should be tagged for identification by the handling agents. Initial calculations based on the body postures observed during performance of several of the tasks indicate that repeated lifting of weights in excess of about 45 pounds would injure most of the working population at some time during their work experience. It would seem prudent to tag bags which exceed a weight of about 40 pounds as this would allow employees at any point in the loading or unloading process to easily identify bags which present a lifting hazard.

  • Conveyors should be installed at the ticketing counters so Customer Service Agents do not need to carry heavy boxes and bags from the counter to the take away conveyor. This is common practice in many airports and greatly minimizes the amount of lifting, twisting, and torso flexions that Customer Service Agents must perform.
 
  • Terminal Agents who unload baggage from the carts for placement onto the loading  conveyor at the plane twist the torso while lifting baggage which can exceed 70 pounds in weight (Figure 4). The carts are often parked such that the loading conveyor is positioned at the mid-point of the cart. This positioning encourages employees to plant themselves in one position while unloading the entire cart. Repeatedly lifting heavy items while twisting the torso increases the risk of development of MSDs of the low back.

 

  • Raise the carousel conveyor such that it is close to the same height as the baggage cart. This would allow employees to transfer baggage from one to the other without the need to bend at the waist and twist while placing the load to the lower level.

  • Heavier bags which have been identified should be loaded onto the plane first such that the amount of lifting and maneuvering of the heaviest items can be minimized. None of the bags tagged as heavy should be lifted onto the top of stacks or lifted to the back of the cargo area.

  • It appears that changing the position of the baggage cart in relation to the position of the aircraft loading conveyor would reduce employee torso twisting. The cart should be stopped such that the conveyor is close to the front of the cart. As can be seen in Figure 6 there is little twisting when employees load the conveyor from the back end of the cart. From this position employees lift the bag and keep it directly in front of their body with little twisting. They then take a step or two forward and place it on the conveyor. If the cart were not pulled so far forward all the items on the cart would be behind the conveyor and all lifts could be made with this technique which reduces the amount of torso twisting.
  • Terminal Agents loading baggage while inside the cargo compartment of the plane must repeatedly lift heavy items with the torso flexed forward and with the arms fully extended away from the body. It is very difficult to attain proper positioning during the loading process inside the plane because of the restricted space limitation within the cargo area which forces employees to perform almost all tasks on their knees. This forces employees to flex the torso forward and to perform extended reaches when placing items at the back of the cargo area and when placing items on top of the stacks which are created during the loading process. Repeatedly lifting heavy items utilizing torso flexions and twisting, and reaching with a load in the hand are hazards which increase the risk of development of (MSDs) of the low back and shoulder.

  • Develop and use a rotation scheme which regularly rotates employees out of tasks which place a greater stress on the body. Tasks such as work within the cargo areas of the plane could be rotated with those which are less stressful such as loading baggage from the conveyor to the cart.

  • The conveyor which moves baggage to the plane's cargo hold should be modified or the loading procedure should be adjusted such that the baggage is moved farther within the interior of the plane. Currently bags are deposited at the outside edge of the opening into the plane which forces employees to reach and pull to get the baggage into the interior. It appears that the current conveyor could be lifted slightly which would allow it to extend farther into the interior of the plane. If this is as high as the conveyor will go a small powered extension should be added to the end of the conveyor to increase the length and thus the ability to extend into the plane.

  • Terminal Agents who move baggage from the carousel to the carts often load the front of the cart first and then must reach over these items to place extra baggage on the back side of the cart. This type of poor planning forces employees to support significant loads in the hands while the arms are fully extended away from the body. Supporting loads in this manner increases the risk of development of MSDs to the shoulder and low back.

Develop an administrative control which would reserve space on carts for bags which are tagged as heavy loads. Light and small bags should be placed at the back of the cart so space at the front of the cart can be saved for those bags which present a greater hazard. All heavy loads should be placed on the bottom, front tier of the carts so the distance they are lifted can be minimized.

General Solutions for All Hazards Listed Above

It is critical that all employees have extensive training on the basics of body biomechanics and the importance of maintaining the body in an ergonomically neutral position while performing these tasks. This type of training will allow employees to perform the task in the safest possible manner and will facilitate their input of possible changes to the work station which may lessen the hazard. Generally, the torso should not be bent forward farther than 6 to 10 degrees from vertical, reaches should be limited to shoulder height vertically and 16 to 18 inches horizontally. Employees should limit torso twisting by moving their feet instead of twisting their backs. The task should be able to be performed with the elbows in close to the torso and the wrist should not be bent either up or down, or to either side. In addition to general training in the basics of biomechanics employees should be trained on the specifics of their particular tasks. They should understand the mechanics and implications of properly adjusting any work fixtures. They should also be aware of the early warning signs of injury and the proper reporting and post reporting procedures to minimize the chance of long term injury.
