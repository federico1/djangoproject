Baggage Handling:
Transfer Between Baggage Cart and Sorting Conveyor

Employees in the baggage sorting area move bags from conveyors to carts for transport to the plane for departure, and from carts to conveyors upon arrival.

  • Baggage Cart

  • Baggage Sorting Conveyor

 

Baggage Cart

Potential Hazard:

  • Extended reaching into the cart to pull bags from the far side of the cart 
    (Fig. 1). 










  • Elevated and extended reaching with arms above the shoulder while lifting bags to elevated locations inside the cart (Fig. 2). These same postures and motions are necessary when unloading.

 
Fig. 1: Reaching
across cart


Fig. 2: Reaching above
shoulder height.

Possible Solutions:

  • Load heavy bags close to the cart opening to minimize heavy lifts with the arms extended. 

  • Establish work practices (or redesign baggage carts)  to prevent stacking bags above shoulder height.

  • For example, limit stacking of baggage in the cart to two bags high. 

  • This may necessitate adding more carts to the string.

  • Consider redesigning new baggage carts to be narrower to reduce the reach with extended arms.

  • Educate employees in proper lifting techniques to keep load close to and in front of the body. Employees should grasp the bottom of the bag when loading to elevated areas rather than using the handle.

  • Use a rotation scheme to rotate employees out of tasks that are particularly stressful. Rotating will distribute the stressful tasks equally.

Baggage Conveyor

Potential Hazard:

  • Lifting from and to the conveyor while bent over with arms fully extended (Fig. 1).

  • Repeated lifting of heavy objects from a low conveyor or from the floor.






  • Repeated twisting of the torso while lifting the baggage, between the cart and the conveyor (Fig. 2). 

Fig. 1: Lift with arms
fully extended.


Fig. 2: Twisting torso.

Possible Solutions:

  • Raise the height of the conveyor to reduce bending and lifting.

  • Park the cart at an angle to minimize twisting when transferring baggage to the loading conveyor (Fig. 3).

  • All baggage marked "heavy" should be lifted using more than one person or with a lifting aid.

  • Educate employees in proper lifting techniques to keep weight close to the body and near its center.

  • Use a rotation scheme to rotate employees out of tasks that are particularly stressful. Rotating will distribute the stressful tasks equally.
